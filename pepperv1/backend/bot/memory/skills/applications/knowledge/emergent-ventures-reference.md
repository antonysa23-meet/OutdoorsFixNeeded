A CONSENSUS VIEW I AGREE WITH

Markets are the most powerful coordination mechanism humanity has ever produced.

This is not contrarian. It is the foundation of everything I believe. The reason a banana costs ten cents is not because someone is brilliant at banana logistics. It is because centuries of evolved incentive structures coordinate millions of people across dozens of countries who will never meet, never speak, and never need to know each other exist. No central planner designed this. No one could. The information required exceeds what any single entity can process. Hayek was right about this and remains right about this.

Where I diverge from the consensus is in asking: why are we not applying this insight to AI? Every company building AI agents is essentially building a central planner. One agent, one set of instructions, one objective function. This is the Soviet model applied to software. It works for small problems the same way central planning works for small economies. It does not scale. The most complex outputs humanity has ever produced, cancer treatments, semiconductor supply chains, the financial system, none of them were designed by a single intelligence. They emerged from incentive structures so nuanced they took centuries to evolve.

THE IDEA

We are building economic environments for AI agents.

The current paradigm treats AI coordination as an engineering problem. Give agents better tools, better prompts, better architectures. This is like trying to make a planned economy work by hiring smarter bureaucrats. The issue is not intelligence. GPT-4 and Claude are already smart enough. The issue is that no single system, no matter how intelligent, can process the distributed information required to produce complex outputs at scale.

Our thesis: the same economic principles that coordinate 8 billion humans can coordinate millions of agents. Not by telling them what to do, but by creating environments where doing the right thing is the profitable thing.

We built Pepper to test this. Pepper is an agent that learns from scratch. No pre-programmed skills. No instruction manuals. Just a memory system, a reinforcement learning loop, and an environment. When it needed to use Blender, nobody told it how. When it needed to navigate Cloudflare's bot detection, nobody taught it the workaround. It figured these out because the system we built gives it a reason to learn.

This is a small proof of concept. What we want to build is much larger.

Imagine a marketplace where agents compete to solve tasks. An agent that writes better marketing copy earns more compute. An agent that finds a faster route through a supply chain gets replicated. An agent that discovers a novel drug interaction gets funded for further research. No one designs these outcomes. They emerge from the incentive structure, the same way no one designs the fact that bananas are cheap.

The practical path: we start with simple economic environments for marketing and content tasks, where the feedback loop is fast and measurable. Revenue from these funds the research into more complex domains. The end state is autonomous construction coordination, drug discovery pipelines, logistics optimization, problems too complex for any single agent but tractable for properly incentivized populations of agents.

This is not a hypothetical. Ole Peters' ergodicity economics shows that time-average growth differs from ensemble-average growth, which explains why cooperation emerges naturally in non-ergodic systems. Geoffrey West's scaling laws demonstrate that cities and organisms follow power-law distributions that emerge from network effects, not design. Our work applies both frameworks to multi-agent AI systems.
