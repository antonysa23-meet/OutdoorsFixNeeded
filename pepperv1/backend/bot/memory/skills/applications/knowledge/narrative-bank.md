## Origin Story (Full)

I'm from South Carolina. My parents weren't ambitious. I wore the same oversized t-shirts as my brothers and had the same plans to attend the local college and work in one of the industrial plants outside our town. I drove by monuments praising the white race. At a Civil War reenactment as a kid, I asked who the good guys were and everyone pointed at the Confederate flag.

Despite my family's beliefs, education offered salvation. It began with an AP History teacher who showed me kindness and humored my ideas with debate. His request for evidence was matched with my declaration: "Once I have more research." That night, I lay awake staring at the bottom of my brother's top bunk and realized that the immigrants and LGBTQ people "ruining" our country was just bigotry. Much of what my parents told me was true was actually baseless.

My freshman summer I felt very depressed. I distinctly remember thinking that just not having been born would have been a relief. I read Ecclesiastes and felt peace in realizing the impermanence of everything. Then my English teacher shared a graduation speech about how we have the choice to think about what we think about. In that classroom of students dozing off, I tucked my head between my legs and smiled. I realized I had control over myself.

My first act of enlightenment was reading my family's bible. King Solomon was the wisest man to live. He had to know what I should make of myself, yet after owning every pleasure, he realized the impermanence of everything, that all would become dust. I laid restlessly atop my itchy carpet for a few days, digesting King Solomon's clarity. I googled "What is the point of life?"

I found people had been asking this question for millennia. First, I found Stoicism. As the words of Aurelius and Epictetus filled my head, I began quoting long-dead men and taking the duties of my household more seriously. I learned to cook a hearty stew over boxed noodles when my father worked late and did both my brothers' laundry while they were in college. As friends invited me to birthday parties, my parents always declined on my behalf, citing a desire to avoid my corruption. I found peace in all that I couldn't control, and I found respect for my parents because of their lack of control over how the world shaped them.

I felt joy in the simple tasks in my life, but as I grew intellectually, I felt the sinking feeling of being surrounded by unreasonable people. I discovered Existentialism. I found myself faced with the weight of my own freedom, that there was no definite action I should take. Each door that knowledge opened left me with the burden of what possibilities lay beyond. I decided that I must choose a purpose for myself.

I transferred to a different school. I started studying physics obsessively. I saw one of my friends begin working hard for the first time and it hit me that the decision to try is a choice you can make at any point.

On the wall in front of my desk I have my greatest desire and greatest fear:

Build cool shit.
2nd Place.

2nd place is the first loser. To me it is the knowledge that one has compromised, that one had the chance to create something great and failed.

## Quantum Physics Lab

Before Rice, I spent a summer at the University of South Carolina's quantum physics lab. On the first day they handed me two 700-page books on quantum and solid-state physics and a 100-page monograph on carbon lattices. I had taken one calculus-based physics course. I had 8 weeks. Those were 70+ hour weeks studying physics while running VASP simulations, waking up at 3am to check if density functional theory calculations were still running, writing up a poster to present alongside PhDs. The department lead said out of all the students in the program, over 100, she thought I had the "most essence of someone who knows what they're doing." That summer taught me that you learn fastest when the problem is too big for you.

- Building the idea maze. Research what others are doing and why before throwing yourself at ideas.
- Do things that don't scale. Look for opportunity and backfill with strategy.
- Do things that are interesting. The best startups will be worked on for a decade.
- Listen to your gut with people. You can tell when someone really cares about building something.
- I learn by touching. The complex neural patterns associated with intuition require the painful experiences of actually putting the rocks in my mouth.

## The Thesis (Economics of AI Coordination)

Every company building AI agents is essentially building a central planner. One agent, one set of instructions, one objective function. This is the Soviet model applied to software. It works for small problems the same way central planning works for small economies. It does not scale. The most complex outputs humanity has ever produced were not designed by a single intelligence. They emerged from incentive structures so nuanced they took centuries to evolve.

The reason a banana costs ten cents is not because someone is brilliant at banana logistics. It is because centuries of evolved incentive structures coordinate millions of people across dozens of countries who will never meet, never speak, and never need to know each other exist. No central planner designed this. No one could. The information required exceeds what any single entity can process. Hayek was right about this.

We are building economic environments where populations of agents compete, cooperate, and specialize through incentive structures. Not by telling them what to do, but by creating environments where doing the right thing is the profitable thing.

## The AI Firm (from Refined Essay)

Marc Andreesen said software is eating the world and our goal is to make firms less like hardware and more like software. Google won search because each query made it smarter. You win at companies because each task execution makes the company more valuable. A competitor can copy code on day one. They can't copy ten thousand failed/successful tasks.

One line: Teach AI to project-manage reality, then own what it learns.

For evolution we need replication, variation, and selection. In business we have the latter two but a company can only grow or die. Corporations grow and coast off inertia until they are eventually consumed, rather than sooner evolving. The solution is pain: short-term feedback and long-term feedback. A human who feels when their leg is broken changes what caused it. Agents need to feel pain too.

## Writing Voice (reference for all applications)

From the "Write like Adam" Claude.ai project:
- Short declarative sentences that build momentum
- No em dashes, ever
- No hedging language ("I believe," "I think"). Claims stated with conviction
- Moves fluidly between close reading/specific evidence and interpretive claim without heavy signposting
- Opens with provocative or contrarian observation, then builds specific, reasoned argument
- Frames through what should exist versus current reality
- Blends philosophical references (Stoic, existentialist, Buddhist) with casual, sometimes blunt language
- Avoids formulaic phrasing common to LinkedIn or generic professional writing
- Prefers prose that earns its conclusions through concrete reasoning rather than assertion alone
- Active, precise construction. Cut vague or passive language.
